# Incoterms 2020 Reference

Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) are a set of eleven three-letter rules published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC) that define the respective obligations of sellers and buyers in international and domestic sale of goods contracts. The current version is **Incoterms 2020**, in effect from 1 January 2020. The previous versions (2010, 2000, etc.) remain valid if the contract explicitly references them, so always check which version the contract cites.

Each Incoterm allocates four things: **who arranges and pays for transport**, **who bears risk and at what point risk transfers**, **who arranges and pays for insurance**, and **who handles export and import clearance**. Incoterms do not govern title transfer, payment terms, or remedies for breach — those live in the sale contract itself.

## The two groups

Incoterms 2020 separates rules by transport mode. Using a sea-only term (FAS, FOB, CFR, CIF) for a containerized or multimodal shipment is one of the most common and costly mistakes in international trade, because risk transfer points defined around ship's rail make no sense when containers sit at inland terminals for days.

**Rules for any mode of transport (seven rules):** EXW, FCA, CPT, CIP, DAP, DPU, DDP.

**Rules for sea and inland waterway transport only (four rules):** FAS, FOB, CFR, CIF. Use these only when goods are loaded directly onto a vessel — traditional bulk, break-bulk, and some project cargo. For containerized cargo handed over to a carrier at an inland point or at a container terminal, use FCA (equivalent of FOB) or CPT/CIP (equivalents of CFR/CIF).

## Rules for any mode of transport

### EXW — Ex Works (named place)

The seller makes goods available at its own premises (or another named place — factory, warehouse, etc.) and does nothing further. The buyer bears all costs and risks from that point, including loading onto the first vehicle, export clearance, main carriage, import clearance, and delivery. EXW represents the minimum obligation for the seller. Use it for domestic-style transactions where the buyer is in a strong position to handle export formalities; it is generally unsuitable for genuine cross-border trade because the buyer, as a foreign party, may struggle with export clearance in the seller's country. **FCA at the seller's premises is almost always a better choice than EXW for international shipments.**

### FCA — Free Carrier (named place of delivery)

The seller delivers goods, cleared for export, to a carrier or another person nominated by the buyer at a named place. Risk transfers when goods are handed over to the carrier (or placed at the buyer's disposal, loaded on the seller's transport, if the named place is the seller's premises). The seller handles export clearance; the buyer handles main carriage, insurance (if any), import clearance, and onward delivery. FCA is the workhorse term for containerized and multimodal trade.

A notable 2020 addition: when the contract requires a letter of credit payable against an on-board Bill of Lading, the parties may agree that the buyer instructs the carrier to issue an on-board BL to the seller after loading, even though under FCA the seller's risk has already passed. This resolves a long-standing conflict between container shipping practice and LC documentary requirements.

### CPT — Carriage Paid To (named place of destination)

The seller delivers to a carrier of its own choosing, pays for carriage to the named destination, and handles export clearance. **Risk, however, transfers when the seller hands the goods to the first carrier, not at the destination.** This cost-risk split is the single most misunderstood feature of the C-terms (CPT, CIP, CFR, CIF) and deserves attention during contract negotiation. The buyer handles insurance (there is none required under CPT), import clearance, and any unloading at destination unless otherwise agreed.

### CIP — Carriage and Insurance Paid To (named place of destination)

As CPT, but the seller additionally procures cargo insurance in the buyer's favor. Incoterms 2020 raised the minimum insurance requirement under CIP from Institute Cargo Clauses (C) to **Institute Cargo Clauses (A)** — that is, "all risks" coverage at 110% of contract value in the currency of the contract. This is now materially different from CIF, which still requires only ICC (C) as the baseline. As with CPT, risk transfers at the first carrier, not at destination.

### DAP — Delivered at Place (named place of destination)

The seller delivers goods, ready for unloading, at the named destination. Seller bears all costs and risks until that point: export clearance, main carriage, and any transit. **Unloading at destination is the buyer's responsibility.** The buyer also handles import clearance and import duties and taxes. DAP suits situations where the buyer wants a delivered solution but has local customs capability.

### DPU — Delivered at Place Unloaded (named place of destination)

DPU replaced DAT (Delivered at Terminal) in Incoterms 2020 and broadened the concept: delivery can now be at any named place, not just a terminal. It is the **only Incoterm under which the seller is responsible for unloading at destination.** The seller bears costs and risks through unloading at the named place; the buyer handles import clearance and duties. Because unloading risk sits with the seller, the seller must be confident it can actually unload at the named place (equipment, access, permissions).

### DDP — Delivered Duty Paid (named place of destination)

The maximum obligation for the seller. The seller delivers goods to the named destination cleared for import, with all duties, taxes, and import charges paid. The buyer only needs to unload (unless otherwise agreed — if the parties want the seller to unload, DPU is actually the cleaner term). DDP is attractive to the buyer but operationally hard for the seller, because the seller typically must be registered for VAT or other local taxes in the destination country to act as importer of record. Many sellers quote DDP loosely and then discover they cannot actually clear import themselves — a frequent source of disputes. Use DDP only when the seller genuinely has the capability or a reliable agent.

## Rules for sea and inland waterway transport

### FAS — Free Alongside Ship (named port of shipment)

The seller delivers when the goods are placed alongside the ship (on the quay, barge, or in a lighter) at the named port of shipment. Risk transfers at that point. The seller handles export clearance; the buyer handles loading on board, main carriage, import clearance, and onward movement. FAS is used for heavy lift, project cargo, and bulk commodities where alongside delivery is commercially meaningful.

### FOB — Free on Board (named port of shipment)

The seller delivers when the goods are on board the vessel nominated by the buyer at the named port of shipment. Risk transfers once goods are on board. Seller handles export clearance; buyer handles main carriage, insurance, import clearance, and onward costs. FOB is one of the most widely used and most widely misused Incoterms — buyers routinely request "FOB Shanghai" for container shipments when FCA would be correct. Under container shipping, goods are handed to the carrier at a container yard well before vessel loading, and a strict reading of FOB leaves the seller at risk during stacking and waiting time that it no longer controls.

### CFR — Cost and Freight (named port of destination)

The seller pays freight to the named destination port but risk transfers when goods are on board at the port of shipment — the same cost/risk split as CPT, applied to sea freight. No insurance obligation; the buyer bears insurable risk during main carriage unless it arranges its own cover.

### CIF — Cost, Insurance and Freight (named port of destination)

As CFR, plus the seller procures marine cargo insurance in the buyer's favor. The minimum cover required under CIF remains **Institute Cargo Clauses (C)** — a restricted, named-perils policy — at 110% of contract value. This is significantly less cover than CIP's ICC (A). Buyers who want "all risks" under a sea shipment should either negotiate for ICC (A) within CIF (permitted, but non-default) or switch the incoterm entirely. Risk transfer, like CFR, is at on-board loading, not destination.

## Practical selection guidance

Start from two questions. First, who is operationally better placed to arrange transport and clearance at each leg — the seller in the origin country or the buyer in the destination country? Second, where should risk during main carriage sit, and who will insure it?

For most containerized cross-border sales, the useful set is **FCA, CPT, CIP, DAP, DDP**, matched by the buyer-managed equivalents or chosen based on who has the freight leverage. For bulk and break-bulk sea freight, **FAS, FOB, CFR, CIF** remain the working set.

A buyer negotiating FOB should verify that the goods are truly loaded onto a vessel rather than into a container handed over inland. A seller quoting DDP should verify it can legally act as importer of record in the destination country. A buyer expecting "all risks" insurance under CIF should explicitly upgrade to ICC (A) in the contract — the default is not enough.

## Changes from Incoterms 2010 worth remembering

- **DAT → DPU**, with delivery now at any named place rather than a terminal.
- **CIP insurance raised to ICC (A)**; CIF unchanged at ICC (C).
- **FCA now supports on-board Bill of Lading** by agreement, resolving the FCA/LC conflict.
- **Security-related obligations clarified** — both parties now have explicit duties around security information.
- **Seller/buyer's own transport** is now explicitly contemplated (previously the rules implicitly assumed a third-party carrier).
