# Expert Interview Protocol (Semi-Structured) — v3.0

**Topic:** Decision criteria for selecting laptops for office workers in Vietnam (price range 20–25 million VND)
**Participants:** B2B procurement specialists, retail sales advisors, IT support technicians and help-desk staff, and experienced users
**Estimated duration:** 20–30 minutes · **Core questions:** 13 (with follow-up probes)
**Role in the study:** A qualitative Delphi round (Round 1) that validates and refines the fourteen criteria before they are quantified through AHP
**Methodological basis:** The Interview Protocol Refinement framework (Castillo-Montoya, 2016), the integrated Delphi–AHP approach (Al Hazza et al., 2022), and cognitive probing techniques (Willis, 2005)

## Part 0 — Greeting and Informed Consent

*Note to the interviewer: read or paraphrase the passage below before starting.*

"Hello, and thank you for taking the time to speak with me today. I am [name], a student at [institution]. Our team is conducting an academic study to build a set of decision criteria for choosing laptops for office workers in the 20–25 million VND range. We have compiled a list of criteria drawn from international research, and this conversation is meant to test and extend that list against your practical experience.

The interview should take about 20 to 30 minutes. Let me note a few points about research ethics so that you can speak freely. Any personally identifying information will be kept anonymous and used only for academic purposes. This conversation will not be audio-recorded; I will only take written notes of the main points. You may decline to answer any question, or stop the interview at any time, without giving a reason.

Do you agree to take part, and may I begin?"

## Part 1 — Warm-Up and Contextualizing Experience (3 questions)

*Purpose: to build rapport and ground the expert's perspective in a concrete case rather than an abstract opinion, moving from the specific to the general as the IPR framework recommends.*

**Question 1.** Please think back to the most recent time you advised on or selected a laptop for an office worker in this price range. What was the first thing you considered, or the first thing the customer asked about?
- *Probe (recall):* Could you walk me through that specific case?

**Question 2.** Compared with student or gaming customers, what particular difficulties or concerns do office workers bring that you notice most often?
- *Probe (process):* How did you come to notice that — from after-sales feedback, or during the consultation itself?

**Question 3.** In your observation, how long does someone in this group usually take to reach a decision, and who has the greatest influence on their final choice?

---

## Part 2 — Stimulus-Based Critique of the Criteria (4 questions)

*Purpose: to compare the fourteen academic criteria against practice. The interviewer reads the matching stimulus card (Appendix B) before each question, then asks for a response. The fourteen criteria fall into three groups: (I) hardware and performance — CPU, RAM, storage, and GPU; (II) mobility and durability — battery, weight, design, and build; (III) experience and trust — display, keyboard, ports, brand, and after-sales service; alongside price as the underlying criterion.*

**Group I — Hardware and Performance** *(read Card A)*

**Question 4.** In the 20–25 million VND range, office customers often weigh a machine with a basic discrete GPU against one that puts the budget into a stronger CPU or more RAM. In your experience, which way do they lean, and why?
- *Probe (process):* What case or reasoning led you to that view?

**Group II — Mobility and Durability** *(read Card B)*

**Question 5.** International research tends to rank battery life very highly, yet offices in Vietnam almost always have power outlets. In that setting, do you find customers weigh battery life or the machine's weight more heavily?
- *Probe:* Is there a specific situation that shaped your view?

**Group III — Experience and Trust** *(read Card C)*

**Question 6.** Suppose there are two machines at the same price: machine A has stronger specifications but a lesser-known brand, while machine B is slightly weaker but from a familiar brand. Which one do you observe office customers leaning toward?
- *Probe:* How far do warranty and after-sales service enter into that decision?

**Question 7.** Analysis of user reviews shows that fan noise and a bulky charger draw a great many complaints, yet they have little effect on the final purchase decision. Does that match what you see, and why do you think that gap exists?

---

## Part 3 — Exploring the Vietnamese Context (2 questions)

*Purpose: to surface locally specific criteria that international research may overlook. Ask openly, without prompting.*

**Question 8.** From your practical experience, is there any factor specific to the working environment in Vietnam that shapes the laptop purchase decision and that international research may not have addressed?
- *Fallback prompt (only if the expert is stuck):* for example, commuting by motorbike, the hot and humid climate, or the software ecosystem in local workplaces.

**Question 9.** Looking back at the groups of criteria we discussed, is there any criterion you would add, or any you would remove, when evaluating laptops for office workers? Why?

---

## Part 4 — Trade-Off Scenario and Ranking (2 questions)

*Purpose: to activate realistic comparative reasoning and generate seed data for the pairwise comparisons in AHP (Phase 4).*

**Question 10.** Let me offer a hypothetical. Two machines cost the same 22 million VND: machine A has about ten hours of battery but weighs 1.8 kg with an ordinary display, while machine B has about six hours of battery, weighs 1.3 kg, and has a noticeably better display. If an office customer asked for advice, which would you lean toward, and why?

**Question 11.** If a customer told you they only needed the three most important criteria secured, with the rest kept flexible, which three criteria would you treat as the priorities?

---

## Part 5 — Closing the Interview (2 questions)

*Purpose: member-checking and closure.*

**Question 12.** Let me summarize the three main points I noted from you: [interviewer states two or three points]. Have I understood you correctly?

**Question 13.** Is there anything about choosing office laptops that I did not ask about, but that you consider important and would like to add?

"Thank you again for your time and for sharing such practical perspectives. The data from this conversation will be coded, combined with the other interviews, and used to build the laptop evaluation model in the next phase. I wish you a productive day."

## Appendix A — Interview Objective and Research Question Matrix

The study's formal research questions are threefold: RQ1 concerns identifying the appropriate criteria, RQ2 concerns the weight of those criteria, and RQ3 concerns the optimal laptop choice. Because the expert interview serves as a qualitative Delphi round, it addresses RQ1 directly and provides seed qualitative data for RQ2. The four interview objectives (IO) below act as an intermediate layer between the interview questions and the research questions.

| Interview objective (IO) | Interview questions | Research question |
|---|---|---|
| IO-A — Relative importance of criteria in practice | Q1, Q2, Q11 | RQ1 (and seed data for RQ2) |
| IO-B — Gap between theory and Vietnamese practice | Q4, Q5, Q6, Q7 | RQ1 |
| IO-C — Criteria specific to the Vietnamese context | Q3, Q8, Q9 | RQ1 |
| IO-D — Trade-off behavior among criteria | Q10 | RQ2 (seed for AHP pairwise comparison) |
| Member-checking procedure | Q12, Q13 | Ensures data reliability |

## Appendix B — Stimulus Cards

*The interviewer reads the matching card to the expert, or shows it, before asking each Part 2 question. The aim is to present an academic finding as material for critique without leading the answer.*

**Card A — Hardware and Performance.** In their study of the laptop market, Maghsoudi et al. (2026) found that CPU and RAM were the two criteria experts rated most important, each scoring 0.8333, whereas the GPU scored 0.6083 and ranked tenth in the TOPSIS results. The authors also observed that professional users focus mainly on display and keyboard, while GPU performance is chiefly the concern of gaming users, so a discrete GPU tends to become over-specification for office needs.

**Card B — Mobility and Durability.** In their study of mobile devices, Lam et al. (2023) ranked battery life as the most important factor within the technical-specification group, with a weight of 0.2841. Across all criteria taken together, however, price held first place and battery life came second. This finding raises the question of how battery life and weight compare in a particular working context.

**Card C — Experience and Trust.** In their study of the laptop selection problem, Sönmez Çakır and Pekkaya (2020) identified brand image as the most important criterion and, at the same time, as a transition criterion that governs how buyers perceive both price and quality. On after-sales service, Maghsoudi et al. (2026) reported that warranty scored 0.6667 in importance yet ranked only eighth in the TOPSIS results. The same study found that fan noise drew 85.1% negative feedback and the charger drew 68.33%, while their influence on the purchase decision stood at just 0.25; the authors reasoned that a feature provoking a strong emotional response does not necessarily drive the purchase decision.
